Can synthetic data replace real credit data?

Banks use your financial history to decide whether to approve loans. But that data is sensitive, legally restricted, and hard to share. This project tests whether AI-generated fake data can train credit scoring models just as effectively — without exposing anyone's real information.

The Problem
Credit scoring models need large, high-quality datasets — but privacy laws, competitive sensitivity, and regulation make real consumer data hard to access. Synthetic data is a proposed solution, but most research only checks if it predicts well, ignoring whether it's fair and explainable.
The Method
A GAN-based model called CTGAN was trained on real credit data to generate artificial records that statistically mimic the real thing. Credit scoring models were then trained on this synthetic data and tested on real data — a framework called TSTR (Train-Synthetic-Test-Real).
The Verdict
Synthetic data can preserve predictive accuracy — especially on simpler datasets. But its impact on fairness and explainability is unpredictable and depends heavily on which model you use and how complex the data is. It is not a plug-and-play replacement.

Key Findings

01
Accuracy drops with complexity
On the simpler GMSC dataset (11 features), synthetic-trained models stay competitive. On the complex Home Credit dataset (45 features), XGBoost drops from AUC 0.752 to 0.681. The more complex the data, the harder it is to fake convincingly.
02
Logistic Regression surprises
On GMSC, the model trained on synthetic data actually outperforms the real-data model (AUC 0.857 vs 0.833). Synthetic data may remove statistical noise that confuses simpler linear models — a genuinely counterintuitive finding.
03
Fairness is unpredictable
On Home Credit, XGBoost and Logistic Regression become fairer when trained on synthetic data. But EBM becomes less fair. There is no consistent direction — which model you use determines whether synthetic data helps or hurts fairness.
04
Models agree on what matters
SHAP analysis shows real and synthetic models rank the same features as most important. Revolving credit utilisation (GMSC) and external credit scores (Home Credit) dominate in both training regimes — explainability is the most robust dimension.
05
Differences are statistically real
Bootstrap testing across 1,000 iterations confirms all AUC differences are significant (p<0.0001). The gaps between real and synthetic performance are not noise — they are consistent and reproducible.
06
Synthetic data needs validation
This study found that synthetic data cannot simply replace real data without careful testing. Under the EU AI Act and FCA guidance, credit scoring is high-risk AI — any training data substitution must be validated across all three dimensions: accuracy, fairness, and explainability.
